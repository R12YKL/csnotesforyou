Introduction to Computer Science

Learning Outcomes

  • Understand the scope and relevance of computer science in modern society
  • Identify the three strands of the Leaving Certificate Computer Science curriculum
  • Learn fundamental computing concepts and their real-world applications
  • Recognize the intersection of theory and practical programming

What is Computer Science?

Computer Science is the study of computation, automation, and information. It encompasses:

  • The theory of how computers process information
  • The design and implementation of practical computing systems
  • The application of computational thinking to solve problems
  • The impact of technology on society, culture, and the economy

Unlike information technology or coding alone, computer science explores the fundamental principles that underpin all computing.

The Three Strands of the Curriculum

Strand 1: Representation and Storage

How information is represented and stored in computer systems:

  • Number systems (binary, decimal, hexadecimal)
  • Data representation (text, images, sound, video)
  • Storage mechanisms and memory organization
  • File systems and data structures

Strand 2: Computer Systems

Understanding how computers work at hardware and software levels:

  • Computer architecture (Von Neumann model)
  • Operating systems and their functions
  • Networks and communication protocols
  • Hardware components and their roles

Strand 3: Computational Thinking

Problem-solving and algorithm design:

  • Decomposition: breaking problems into manageable parts
  • Pattern recognition and abstraction
  • Algorithm design and analysis
  • Programming and implementation

Key Concepts

Algorithm: A step-by-step procedure for solving a problem or completing a task. Algorithms are the foundation of all computer programs.

Data: Raw facts and figures without context. Computer science deals with how data is collected, stored, processed, and presented.

Information: Data that has been processed, organized, and presented in a meaningful way to support decision-making.

Computational Thinking: A problem-solving approach that involves decomposition, pattern recognition, abstraction, and algorithm design.

Program: A set of instructions written in a programming language that tells a computer what to do. Programs translate algorithms into executable code.

Why Study Computer Science?

  • Career Opportunities: Computing is one of the fastest-growing sectors with diverse career paths
  • Problem-Solving Skills: Computational thinking applies to all disciplines
  • Understanding Modern Society: Technology impacts every aspect of our lives
  • Digital Literacy: Essential skill for the 21st century
  • Innovation: Computing enables solutions to complex real-world problems

Course Structure

The Leaving Certificate Computer Science course consists of:

  • Written Examination (80%): Tests theoretical knowledge and problem-solving
  • Practical Project (20%): Demonstrates practical programming and design skills

Topics covered include hardware, software, algorithms, data representation, networking, and applications of computing in real-world scenarios.

Quick Tips for Success

  • Balance theoretical understanding with practical coding experience
  • Practice solving problems step-by-step using computational thinking
  • Understand the "why" behind algorithms and concepts, not just the "how"
  • Apply what you learn to real-world problems
  • Review regularly and make connections between topics
